Bio

Esmé Belle, (Esmeralda Hernandez), is a multidisciplinary artist born and raised in Long Island, New York. Her work merges mundane moments captured from the countries she’s traveled to, into surrealistic paintings, drawings, photography, film, animation, and ceramic sculptures.

Esmé has exhibited across New York, including the Student Art Show at Suffolk County Community College, Best of SUNY art exhibition (Albany, NY), and Abstract Arrangement Multi Media Showcase (Brooklyn, NY).

Esmé has also done over a dozen promotional short films for fashion, beauty, and perfume brands such as Gap, Versace, Prada Beauty, Givenchy, and Moschino. Her practice reflects a deep connection to her Salvadoran heritage and the everyday poetry found in domestic life, using art as a bridge between personal narrative and collective experience.

Artist Statement

Before I ever called myself an artist, I was a kid hunched over the kitchen table, turning glitter, tape, and old photos into gifts that meant more than anything I could buy. I think that’s still what drives me today, turning ordinary moments into something people can feel, something that quietly says, I see you.

A lot of my work circles back to memory, the kind you don’t even realize you’re carrying. The colors and patterns from my Salvadoran household, the smell of food during late-night painting sessions, the way a single photograph can unlock an entire history. Hands appear in my work again and again, maybe because they’re the first thing I notice about people, or because so much of our lives depends on what we hold and what we let go.

Self-portraiture has become another thread in my practice; partly because I’m always available as a subject, but mostly because studying myself becomes a way of confronting emotions I would otherwise avoid.

My art spans oil painting, charcoal drawing, ceramics, animation, and mixed media, but the story always comes first. Whether I’m painting on a salvaged blanket, building a ceramic planter layered with graffiti motifs, or reconstructing memories through charcoal, each piece begins with a small personal truth before expanding into something shared. I want people to feel calm, even cozy, when they look at my work, as if they’ve stepped into a familiar memory or a fever dream they half-remember.

If my work says anything about me, I hope it reflects how deeply I value the people I come from, the places that shaped me, and the stories that keep pulling me back to create again.
